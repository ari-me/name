Kitchen Tips and Tricks


The kitchen tips and tricks page contains a few cooking advices Arwen learned throughout her learning experience. These tips and tricks can help make one's cooking experience even a bit simpler. Whether you're a masterful home cook or a newbie, remember the motto: "there's always more things to learn and ways to improve." Tactics for improving how you keep safe prep, cook, and store food and ways to make kitchen appliances work for you and not the other way around. Read through it and find out which you already knew about!



01

Clean Knives and Surfaces


For safe food preparation, don't cut raw meat, poultry or fish on the same surface or with the same knife without cleaning it thoroughly between uses. Wash your knife and cutting board with soap and hot water, or change the knife or board after each use.

02

Stop boiling water from overflowing


Place a wooden spoon lengthwise on top of the pot, and the water will not boil over. This works because the wood acts as a water repellant for the bubbles which stops it from rising, and the spoon being of a cooler temperature that when the bubbles meet the spoon, it condenses back into water.

03

Read the whole recipe before starting


It seems like common sense but is actually forgotten a lot. It's important to read the whole recipe from ingredients to the cooking process so that you know the basic structure and how it's made. It also helps if there are ingredients divided into portions that aren't shown in the ingredients section.

04

Honey! Honey! Honey!


Honey is a part of your ingredients and it's sticking to your measuring materials? Well, try coating the measuring spoon or cup with a teaspoon of oil before putting in the honey. It should slide right out!
Crystalized honey? place the container in a bowl of hot water until the honey is smooth again. Alternatively, you could remove the lid and microwave the jar in 30-second intervals.

05

06

Is your pan hot enough for cooking?


Check if your pot is ready for cooking with the water test! Heat up your pan as usual and add a bit of water to the pan. If the water sits in the pan it's not yet ready. If it sizzles and evaporates immediately, it's too hot. If it disperses into tiny beads of water forming into one ball that roll around the bottom of the pan, it's ready and you can wait for the water to evaporate then add your oil.

07

Peeling Boiled Eggs


There are many tricks to getting the perfect boiled eggs. But there is one trick that worked the most for me. Before boiling your eggs, lightly tap the egg with a spoon until you hear a crack sound. That shows that the membrane of your egg is cracked giving more space when you open it. When transferring your boiled eggs, crack them while it's in cold water, give it some time, and then hit it and peel as usual. Take note that despite it being easier, it may still take some time to get used to and not all eggs have to be perfect :)

08

Softening Butter


Forgot to take butter out of the refrigerator in advance?I know 3 ways to soften it quick!
1 Get a cup big enough to hold the stick of butter. Put boiling water into that cup. After a few minutes, drain the water. Cover the butter with the cup, the heat from the hot water will soften the butter.
2 Cut the sticks into pieces and set out on a counter. In 10 to 15 minutes, you'll be good to go.
3 Micorwave the cut pieces on low in 20-second intervals until it's malleable (not mushy).

09

Cutting Bell Peppers


It's bothersome when bell pepper seeds get everywhere while cutting don't they? Well, try flipping the bell pepper stem side down and cut the bell pepper by the lines. Peel those parts from the stem, and there you got the bell peppers away from the seeds easily.

10

Keeping Deep Fried Food Crispy


Have you fried something to crispy perfection, only to find later that they've turned soggy? The residual heat of fried food becomes trapped under the food and creates steam, transforming crispy to crummy. Instead of a plate, put your freshly fried food on a cooling rack with a baking sheet on the bottom to collect the dripping oil. With room for air to circulate, fried morsels maintain their satisfying crunch until dinner.
